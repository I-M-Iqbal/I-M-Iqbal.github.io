There have been several legal developments following the UKSC's For Women judgement which ruled that the meaning of Sex in the Equality Act 2010 refers to biological sex. For a detailed discussion of this judgment, please see: https://quilletsofthelaw.com/for-women-ltd-v-the-scottish-ministers-explained/. One such development is that the Good Law Project (GLP) filed a judicial review case against the EHRC's interim guidance about the judgment. A judgment was handed down in the case by Mr Jonathan Swift KC in February 2026. This post aims, like my last post about these issues, to explain the judgement and what comes next. 

## A quick note about language:

As mentioned in my prior post, the law often uses language which doesn't not reflect the language trans people would use to refer to themselves. Where such language is used in this post, it is for legal accuracy alone. Outside of such circumstances language which is accurate has been used, the language trans people would prefer to use has been used and alternative language is solely used to ensure readers of this post can understand the language being used by the court.

A full glossary of the language used in this post can be found here:https://quilletsofthelaw.com/for-women-ltd-v-the-scottish-ministers-explained/ but the three most important terms are defined below:

Sex: Sex assigned at birth.  
Gender: is used to refer to how a person identifies. As mentioned in the prior post, the law uses the terms gender and sex alternatively, so for the sake of clarity the term gender will only be used where necessary in this post.  
Acquired Gender: The gender a trans person identifies as.

## The high court judgment in the Good Law Project v Equality and Human Rights Commission:

This case concerns the interim guidance issued by the Equality and Human Rights Commission (EHRC) following the For women supreme court decision. This guidance was removed from the commission's website, which now advises organisations to seek specific legal advice.[^1] It was recently announced that the final guidance is with the Minister for Women and Equalities and further details will follow in May 2026.[^2] A further blog post will be written about the new guidance at that stage. 

The conclusions in the For Women judgement significantly impacts the Code of practice for service providers, which has been issued in 2011 following the passage of the Equality Act 2010. The prior code states: 

>'If a service provider provides single or separate sex services for women and men, or provides services differently to women and men, they should treat transsexual people according to the gender role in which they present. However, the Act does permit the service provider to provide a different service or exclude a person from the service who is proposing to undergo, is undergoing or who has undergone gender reassignment. This will only be lawful where the exclusion is a proportionate means of achieving a legitimate' aim.[^3] 

The reason the For Women case impacts upon this guidance is if the definition of sex under the Equality Act refers to biological sex, then facilities that allow trans people to use the facilities of their acquired gender would no longer be considered single sex.
